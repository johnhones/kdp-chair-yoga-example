Marketing Strategy
Step 1 - Add Your Keywords to KDP

In a saturated niche, keyword strategy is even more important than in an open one. Amazon uses your keywords to decide which searches your book appears in. The right terms can steer you away from the most heavily contested pages and towards searches where you have a realistic chance of being seen.

✓
Avoid broad terms until your review count is established
KDP gives you 7 keyword fields. In this niche, broad terms like “chair yoga for seniors” are dominated by books with 1,600+ reviews. Target these only once you have at least 30 to 50 reviews. Until then, focus on specific long-tail terms where competition is far thinner.
✓
Lead with sub-niche terms
Specific phrases like “chair yoga for arthritis,” “seated yoga for limited mobility,” and “gentle chair exercises elderly” attract buyers with a specific need and face far less competition than the primary category terms. These are your best starting keywords.
✓
Use the recommended keywords in this audit
The keywords section of this audit contains research-backed terms with lower competition. These have been selected specifically to give a new book a realistic entry point. Copy them directly into your KDP keyword fields.
↓
Step 2 - Analyse Your Competing Books

In a saturated niche, understanding the competition is not optional. The dominant books have earned their position through years of sales, reviews, and optimisation. Study them carefully before making any investment in promotion.

✓
Study the top two books in particular
Chair Yoga for Seniors and Chair Yoga for Weight Loss together account for the majority of revenue in this niche. Buy them, read them, and understand exactly what they do well. Look for what they miss or what could be genuinely improved. That gap is your only realistic angle of differentiation.
✓
Read critical reviews of the leaders
Negative reviews of the dominant books tell you what buyers feel is missing. If multiple reviews mention a lack of progression, or insufficient focus on specific conditions, that may be a gap you can target with a sub-niche title rather than a direct competitor.
✓
Assess realistically whether you can outcompete
If you cannot identify a meaningful way to differentiate from books with 1,600+ reviews and years of sales history, consider whether the sub-niche pivot in this audit is the more commercially rational path forward.
↓
Step 3 - Get Some Ratings and Reviews

In a saturated niche, reviews are not just helpful. They are the single most critical factor that determines whether your book can compete at all. A book with fewer than 20 reviews next to competitors with 1,600+ will not convert, regardless of how good the content is.

Building a review base before any paid promotion is not optional in this niche. It is a prerequisite.

✓
Ask for a rating or review inside your book
Add a genuine request at the back and in the middle of your book. Many readers never reach the final pages. Keep it natural and never ask for a 5-star review specifically as Amazon takes a dim view of this.
✓
Aim for at least 20 to 30 reviews before running any ads
In a saturated niche, 15 reviews is not enough. Aim for 20 to 30 before spending on advertising. Shoppers compare options closely in this category and will almost always choose the book with hundreds of reviews over a newcomer with fewer than 30.
✓
Use an ARC service to build social proof before launch
ARC (Advance Review Copy) services such as BookSirens and Hidden Gems connect authors with readers who have opted in to receive free books in exchange for honest reviews. These services are legitimate, widely used, and fully compliant with Amazon’s terms of service. Do not launch into this competitive niche without them.
✓
Reach out to physiotherapists, yoga instructors, and senior fitness communities
For a book aimed at seniors and those with limited mobility, there are professionals and communities who work with exactly this audience every day. Yoga instructors, physiotherapists, senior centre coordinators, and occupational therapists are all potential early reviewers who can provide credible, relevant feedback and help build the social proof the book needs to compete.

For publishing support and strategy, communities like the 20Booksto50K Facebook group and the Alliance of Independent Authors are excellent free resources used by professional self-publishers worldwide.
↓
Step 4 - Consider a Hardback Edition

In a saturated niche where every visual detail matters, a hardback edition can help your book stand out in Amazon search results. The top-performing books in this category are paperbacks, but a hardback signals premium quality at a glance and may justify a higher price point that improves your per-unit royalty margin.

✓
Price it to reflect the premium format
KDP Print books have a minimum list price based on page count and trim size. The royalty is 60% of the list price minus the printing cost. A hardback at a higher price point can deliver stronger per-sale royalties than a paperback competing on price in a crowded market.
✓
Exercise books are reference books. Seniors want them in print
Readers doing chair yoga keep the book nearby during practice. A physical book that stays open on a table or chair is a functional advantage over a Kindle edition. Both paperback and hardback serve this use case better than digital.
✓
Hardback will not overcome the review barrier alone
Be realistic. A hardback edition increases your options and your per-unit revenue, but it does not resolve the core challenge in this niche: a review gap of 1,600+ against new entrants with zero reviews. Combine format strategy with the sub-niche approach in the Additional Opportunity section of this audit.

Step 5 - Sense Check Before You Spend

This is one of the most competitive niches in the Amazon fitness category. The incumbents have years of sales history and over 1,600 reviews each. Before spending any money on advertising, be honest about whether your book offers something genuinely different from what is already there, and whether you have the review base to convert browsers into buyers.

Spending money on ads in a saturated niche without reviews is the fastest way to lose your entire budget.

↓
Step 6 - Amazon Advertising

Amazon Advertising can work in saturated niches but the conditions must be right. In this niche, where 22+ established titles are already competing for ad placements on core keywords, the cost-per-click is high and conversion rates for new books without reviews are low. Advertising here without at least 20 to 30 reviews is unlikely to be profitable.

If you do advertise, the approach must be disciplined: long-tail only, minimal budget, and results measured weekly.

There is real risk in getting it wrong. Start with $5 per day on long-tail terms only, test the data, and do not scale until your ACoS is within a profitable range.

✓
Understand your ACoS in a high-CPC environment
ACoS stands for Advertising Cost of Sale. In a saturated niche, cost-per-click is significantly higher than in uncrowded niches. Expect CPCs of $0.40 to $0.90 on core terms, which means a low conversion rate (typical for books with few reviews) will produce an unprofitable ACoS. Target long-tail terms where CPC is $0.10 to $0.30.
✓
Optimise before you scale
Pause keywords that spend without converting. Lower bids on high-ACoS terms. In a saturated niche, scaling an unprofitable campaign simply accelerates losses. Do not increase your budget until your ACoS is consistently within your target range.
✓
Scale only what works
Once you find a profitable ACoS that holds consistently, increase your daily budget with confidence. Every dollar you put in returns more than a dollar in royalties. The ceiling is not your budget. It is the total demand for books in the sub-niche you are targeting.

Publishing a book takes the same amount of work regardless of which niche you choose. The difference between a good outcome and a frustrating one is almost entirely determined by where you publish, not how hard you work.

An author entering the dog grooming business niche today faces fewer than 15 direct competitors, most with under 200 reviews. A well-positioned book could reach page one organically with 20 to 30 genuine reviews and a modest ads budget.
An author entering the chair yoga niche today faces 22 established competitors, two of which have over 1,600 reviews and years of sales momentum behind them. The same effort produces a fraction of the result.

Additional Opportunity
The Sub-Niche Pivot: Where the Competition Thins Out
A book specifically for one condition or audience faces a very different competitive landscape. Chair yoga for arthritis, for Parkinson’s disease, for post-surgery recovery, or for wheelchair users: each is a smaller market, but each has far fewer entrenched competitors and a much clearer path to page one visibility.
✓
Sub-niche titles can reach page one much faster
A search for “chair yoga for arthritis” returns significantly fewer results than “chair yoga for seniors.” A well-written, properly optimised book targeting that specific condition could reach page one with 15 to 20 reviews, something that is virtually impossible in the main category today.
✓
The same knowledge, a more focused application
A chair yoga book written specifically for people with arthritis is not a fundamentally different book. It is the same expertise applied with a specific reader in mind. The routines, the guidance, the safety considerations are all things you already know. The difference is in the positioning and the title.
✓
Sub-niche books can coexist with your existing title
A sub-niche book is not a replacement for The Complete Chair Yoga Handbook. It is an additional title that targets a different, less contested search term. Both books can live on Amazon simultaneously, and together they create a small catalogue that compounds visibility over time.

Suggested Sub-Niche Title Ideas

1
Chair Yoga for Arthritis
Targets buyers with a specific condition and far fewer competing titles. The reader is highly motivated and willing to pay for content that speaks directly to their situation.
2
Seated Yoga for Parkinson’s Disease
A medically adjacent sub-niche with very little published competition. Physiotherapists, neurologists, and Parkinson’s support groups are natural promotional partners for this type of content.
3
Gentle Chair Exercises for Post-Surgery Recovery
Targets a specific life event with a strong emotional purchase driver. Readers in post-surgery recovery have a specific, urgent need and low tolerance for generic content. A specialist title stands out immediately.

Quick Wins
If You Are Committed to This Space: What to Do
1
Niche down to a specific condition or audience
Rather than “chair yoga” broadly, target a sub-niche with less competition: chair yoga for arthritis, for Parkinson’s disease, for post-hip-replacement recovery, or for wheelchair users. Each is a smaller market, but one with far fewer entrenched competitors.
2
Build a review acquisition strategy before promoting
Do not promote without a plan to generate the first 20 to 30 reviews quickly. This means identifying beta readers, physiotherapists, yoga instructors, or senior centre coordinators willing to read and review in advance of launch. Zero reviews at launch in a saturated niche is commercially fatal.
3
Budget realistically for Amazon Ads
In a saturated niche, organic visibility is unlikely without advertising. Budget at least $15 to $20/day for the first 60 days post-launch, and expect higher ACoS than in an uncrowded niche. Factor this into any profitability calculation before committing.
4
Consider a sub-niche angle as your highest-leverage move
The same writing effort applied to a more targeted angle — chair yoga for arthritis, Parkinson’s, or post-surgery recovery — places you in a far less contested space. The expertise is transferable. A sharper angle gives your book a clearer path to being discovered.
